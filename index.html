Georgia, 2024

State Election Board decisions, impact by county.

A small board in Atlanta decides how every county in Georgia counts mail ballots, how drop boxes work, and when an election gets certified. We sit through their meetings so you don't have to, and we show you how their choices land in the place you actually live.

Recent meetings

What the board has been working on.

These short recaps are written by the team at Common Cause Georgia, who sit in on every meeting.

Regular meeting January 9, 2024

Voting machines and drop-box hours.

The board signed off on the voting machines for the March primary. Three changes to drop-box hours also came up, and one member said out loud that the public hadn't been given enough time to weigh in.

State Capitol, in person · Quorum met · Concerns noted
Emergency meeting February 12, 2024

Absentee ballot complaints from three counties.

The board called this meeting on less than a day's notice. They reviewed complaints from three counties about how absentee ballots were being handled. The short notice became part of the conversation itself.

Virtual · Quorum met · Flagged
Regular meeting March 20, 2024

Signing off on city election results.

The board certified the results of city elections in four counties. Nothing controversial came up, and no one raised a concern. A quiet meeting.

State Capitol, in person · Quorum met · Clean
Emergency meeting April 22, 2024

Reviewing an audit across several counties.

The board read the findings of an audit that covered a handful of counties. One member said the timing felt off, since the May primary was only weeks away and the public didn't have real room to respond.

What's new this season

Three things you can do here now.

The site finished a quiet round of work this spring. Here's what changed for you, in plain language.

New

Look up your county on its own page.

Type your county into the box below; you'll see the board decisions that touched it, alongside how its voter rolls have moved. No login, no signup, no tracking.

New

Download the whole dataset.

Reporters, researchers, and organizers can grab the full county-level numbers as a spreadsheet. We publish the same file we use ourselves, so what we see is what you see.

New

A written commitment on privacy.

We wrote down, in public, what we will never publish: anything that points to a specific voter. Two separate layers of the system enforce it, so a single mistake can't quietly leak.

Find your county

Look up your county.

The board's rules apply across all of Georgia, but they don't feel the same in every county. A change to drop-box hours hits a county with 600,000 voters differently than one with 12,000. Start by finding where you live.

About this project

A public record of how Georgia runs its elections on a county level, with the intersection of State Election Board decisions.

What we publish

Things that are already public.

Board meetings are open by law. So are the counts of how many people are registered to vote. We just gather it all up in one place so you don't have to chase it down.

What we don't publish

Anything that points to you.

We never share information about individual voters. You'll only see totals, and only at the county level. If you've asked to be left out of public datasets, you're left out of ours too.

How to use this

A starting point, not the last word.

We tell you what happened. What it means is up to you. Reporters, organizers, and folks who just want to keep an eye on things are all welcome here.

Who's behind it

GA Watchdog comes from Palacios Contigo, an Atlanta civil rights and election protection group. Everything you see comes from official sources: filings from the Secretary of State, the board's own public agendas and minutes, and the statewide voter file. Spot something wrong, want your county added, or just have a question? Write to rosario@palacios.community.

When something breaks, we say so

Numbers move; sources change; mistakes happen. When they do, we publish a short note explaining what was wrong, what's correct, and how we know. The original (incorrect) value stays in our records so the change itself is something you can audit. We'd rather be honest about a correction than quietly rewrite history.
